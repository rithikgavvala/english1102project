Controlled of the Uncontrolled

Critical introduction

Since the 1980s, interactivity has permeated all corners of digital culture from hyperlinks to first-person video games. As the affordances of computers evolved to incorporate greater levels of technical complexity, interactivity also took on new, distinct forms to engage users of the vast web. While posting pictures and expressing emotions through short forms of text on social media platforms such as Facebook, Snapchat, and Twitter define our primary form of interaction, it must be noted that the involvement with digital medium can be dated back to the introduction of electronic literature, “[a first-generation digital object] that takes advantage of the capabilities and contexts provided by the stand-alone or networked computer” (Hayles). In other words, similar to hard-copy novels, a story is conveyed; however, the stark differences lies in how the journey is navigated. Electronic literature utilizes various navigational structures including hypertext, images, videos, text input, and touch through mouse-clicks to portray the purpose of a piece. By increasing the level of interactivity within their pieces, an author of electronic literature can increase user engagement because this provides an illusion of control where the user feels as if they contribute to a piece’s meaning.
